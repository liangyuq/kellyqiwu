Artifact 3: How I Explained Neural Networks in the Simplest Way Possible with Help of AI

Introduction: In this artifact, I set out to write the best simple explanation of a neural network in machine learning. I used what I know about communication, learning, and AI to turn a hard idea into something easy to understand.

Step 1: Think About the Audience

The audience is anyone who may click into my portfolio, including people with little or no technical background—like a child, a parent, or someone new to AI. This means I need to keep the message short, simple, and clear. I also want to make it friendly and fun, not like a textbook.

From what I’ve learned about communication and learning, people understand better when:

  • The message is tied to something they already know
  • They can see or imagine what’s being described
  • It uses simple words, short sentences, and real examples

Step 2: Use AI to Help Shape the Message

I used ChatGPT to help me test ideas and come up with possible ways to explain neural networks. I gave it prompts like:

Prompt: "Explain what a neural network is like I’m 10 years old. Use a fun example. Make it short. Avoid tech terms."

It gave me several ideas—some talked about teams of robots, others about helpers solving puzzles. One version compared a neural network to a robot’s brain learning to tell the difference between a cat and a dog. I chose that one because it connects well to a real-world task and builds on something many kids and adults understand: animals and learning from mistakes.

Step 3: Final Explanation

Here is the final version I created:

A neural network is like a robot’s brain that helps it learn from examples. Imagine you want to teach a robot how to tell the difference between a cat and a dog. You show it lots of pictures, and over time, it starts to learn what makes a cat a cat and what makes a dog a dog.

First, the robot looks at the picture. It doesn’t see the picture like we do, but it looks at tiny bits of information, like colors and shapes. This part is called the input layer. It’s like the robot’s eyes.

Next, the robot starts thinking about what it saw. It goes through many small steps that ask questions like: Does this animal have pointy ears? Is the nose small or big? Does it have fur? These thinking steps happen in the hidden layers. Each step helps the robot figure out more about what it’s seeing.

Finally, the robot gives an answer. It says, “I think this is a cat,” or “This looks like a dog.” This part is called the output layer. At the beginning, the robot might get the answer wrong, but each time it makes a mistake, it learns from it. It slowly gets better and makes fewer mistakes.

So, a neural network is a smart system that helps a machine learn, think, and decide, kind of like a tiny brain that learns by practicing over and over again.

Step 4: Why I Chose This Explanation

I chose this explanation because it makes a hard idea feel familiar. Most people understand learning from examples, and many children know how to tell a cat from a dog. Using a robot helps show that machines can “learn” too, in a way that’s close to how people learn. This story uses clear steps (look, think, decide) and avoids tech terms unless they are explained with simple language. It also adds feeling—robots make mistakes and learn, just like people. This builds trust and makes the topic feel less scary.

Reflection

Through this project, I learned how to combine communication and teaching. I used storytelling, plain language, and visual thinking to break down a complex idea. I also saw how AI can be a helpful tool—not just for giving answers, but for shaping and testing ideas. By trying different ways to say the same thing, I got better at seeing what worked and what didn’t.

Value: This artifact shows that I can explain big ideas in simple ways. It reflects my skills in writing, audience thinking, and learning design. These are all key parts of education and instructional work.
